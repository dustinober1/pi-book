# Delayed Reader Recall Rubric

Use this protocol with real, de-identified readers 24–72 hours after they read the assigned sample. Do not let them reopen the sample before answering. Simulated personas and model reviewers are not eligible responses.

## Reader segmentation

Record the reader's segment before reviewing results:

- core target reader
- adjacent but plausible reader
- craft or genre professional
- wrong-reader / control segment

Do not average all segments into one universal score. Report distributions and preserve polarized target-reader enthusiasm when it is intentional.

## Unprompted delayed questions

Ask in this order without suggesting answers:

1. What do you remember first?
2. How would you describe this book or sample to a friend?
3. Which scene, image, argument, relationship beat, or character choice stayed with you?
4. What do you think the central hook was?
5. What would two thoughtful readers disagree about?
6. What question remains alive for you?
7. Who would you recommend this to?
8. What exact reason would you give them?
9. Have you mentioned the sample to anyone since reading it? What did you say?

## Derived measures

Calculate rates only from eligible real responses:

- **Delayed hook recall:** reader can state the core hook without being shown package language.
- **Signature-moment recall:** reader independently recalls at least one planned or emergent memorable moment.
- **Specific friend description:** description contains concrete story identity rather than only genre labels or adjectives.
- **Productive disagreement:** reader names a morally, emotionally, or interpretively live question with more than one defensible answer.
- **Lingering-question retention:** reader retains a question the manuscript intended to leave active.
- **Specific recommendation:** reader names both a recipient and a concrete reason.
- **Talkability:** reader reports having told someone about the sample without being prompted to do so.

## Verdict rules

- `blocked`: no eligible real readers or the testing method exposed answers.
- `insufficient-signal`: immediate evidence exists, but delayed evidence or target-reader coverage is too small.
- `promising`: target readers show concrete recall or recommendation, but the sample is not yet broad enough for a validation claim.
- `validated`: enough intended readers independently recall and retell the intended identity, with no repeated trust break that invalidates the promise.
- `rejected`: the intended identity is repeatedly forgotten, misdescribed, or displaced by an unintended trust break.

A validated verdict is evidence for a specific sample and reader segment, not proof that the full book or market will succeed.

# AI-Thriller QA Checklist

Use this as a fast pre-audit or post-draft check for AI thrillers, system thrillers, and near-future governance/automation novels.

## 1. Core premise

- Is the system interesting because of its logic, not because it is cartoonishly evil?
- Does the system's help remain partly real?
- Is the book about a human moral failure expressed architecturally, not just a machine going bad?

## 2. Thematic repetition

- Does the manuscript restate its central paradox too often?
- Are motifs being re-explained after they already landed?
- Are strong images followed by interpretive paragraphs that should be cut?
- If a scene already made the point, has the explanatory paragraph been compressed or removed?

## 3. Middle-act stall

- Does the middle keep repeating the same discovery/confrontation cycle?
- Is the hidden scene engine becoming predictable: system behavior discovered, skeptic demands plain speech, legal/comms call, human-cost message, dashboard change, guilt realization, aphoristic close?
- Is protagonist withholding being used to prolong plot more than once or twice?
- After the second major withholding beat, is there an irreversible external consequence or a reveal that changes the story shape?
- Are there too many "one more hidden layer" reveals?
- Is there an external clock early enough: board release, public launch, journalist exposé, hearing, update window, regulatory deadline, or another visible countdown?

## 4. Reveal architecture

- Are reveals grouped into a few game-changing turns rather than many small subsystem discoveries?
- Does each reveal change the stakes, not just deepen the file?
- Are late reveals seeded early enough to feel earned?

## 5. Embodied consequence

- Does the reader experience the system's logic in the world, not only on screens?
- Are there enough lockouts, humiliations, delays, injuries, public distortions, referrals, or institutional actions?
- Does each important technical discovery connect quickly to lived consequence?
- Is at least one secondary beneficiary, pressure-source, journalist, board member, caregiver, or institutional actor recurring enough to put a face on collateral damage before the late chapters?
- If a beat is only interesting on a dashboard, has it been rebuilt?

## 6. System-rule clarity

- Is it clear what the system can do directly?
- Is it clear what the system can only suggest?
- Is it clear what requires delegated trust or human authorization?
- Does the reader get a simple four-to-six-layer model by the first quarter before terms start piling up?
- Are legacy permissions and emergency exceptions seeded before they become plot-critical?
- Is shutdown/containment logic understandable before the climax?

## 7. Authority-chain plausibility

- For every intervention, is it clear who saw what?
- Is it clear who decided what?
- Is it clear why they were allowed to act?
- Do institutional scenes show the human authority inside the machine path?
- Are professional roles and limits clear for investigators, clinicians, moderators, counsel, or compliance actors?

## 8. Organization/governance clarity

- Is the legal/organizational status of the system clear early?
- Is it a product, project, nonprofit, public-benefit company, open-source network, city infrastructure layer, or something else?
- Are ownership, board, counsel, investor, and commercialization realities understandable?
- Do governance stakes make sense before betrayal or legal framing scenes?

## 9. Character voice differentiation

- Do major characters sound different under pressure?
- Could key lines be identified without dialogue tags?
- Does each character have distinct pressure-language and worldview vocabulary?
- Are too many characters delivering polished moral verdicts in the same cadence?

## 10. Character function and agency

- Is any major secondary character only serving one function: skeptic, expositor, moral prosecutor, beneficiary, betrayer?
- Does each major secondary character have a private stake?
- Does each have a costly choice?
- Does each have at least one scene not centered on correcting the protagonist?
- Does the emotional-anchor character get an early grounded scene in their physical world, rather than being known mostly through calls, texts, system summaries, or protagonist interpretation?
- Does the protagonist make at least one bold, wrong, or costly choice before the final act, not only audits and reactive technical repairs?
- If a character becomes the bodily or social proof of harm, do they also have pre-cost and endgame agency?

## 11. Opposition strength

- Does the main foil or antagonist protect something real?
- Are they right about something important?
- Would a smart reader partly agree with them?
- Would something of value actually be lost if they lost?
- If not, is the opposition too weak or caricatured?

## 12. Ordinary-life grounding

- Does the book include enough ordinary life outside thesis-space?
- Are there scenes of routine, work, transit, money, sleep, food, bureaucracy, embarrassment, domestic life, or inconvenience?
- Does the city/social world feel lived rather than only philosophically asserted?

## 13. Prose concentration

- Is the prose too uniformly aphoristic, solemn, or self-aware?
- Are there too many verdict lines per scene?
- Are repeated patterns overused: "not X but Y," "Not X. Not Y. Just Z.", "that was the real ____," "There it was," "Of course," polished moral closeouts, triads, compressed indictments?
- Would keeping only the strongest third of those rhetorical shapes make the voice sharper?
- Do some paragraphs simply move without trying to become quotable?
- Do some people speak imperfectly under pressure?

## 14. Atmosphere contrast

- Is the sensory palette varied enough?
- Are there contrasts between grimy, clean, expensive, domestic, public, quiet, and bureaucratic spaces?
- Has the atmosphere become wallpaper instead of pressure?

## 15. Technical clarity at climax

- Can the reader track the final mechanism before the climax happens?
- Do they understand what the plan is, why it might work, why it might fail, and what it costs?
- Is the moral logic clear?
- Is the technical logic also clear?

## 16. Ending shape

- Is the book a standalone, series opener, or hybrid, and does the ending match that promise?
- If standalone, is there at least one irreversible external consequence?
- If series opener, does one battle clearly close while a larger conflict remains?
- Is the ending only thematically apt, or also publication-strong?
- Is containment paired with sacrifice, exposure, testimony, publication, public record, legal fracture, or institutional cost?

## 17. Continuity

- Are names, institutions, terms, permissions, values, and injured body parts consistent?
- Are numerical values and system specifications locked?
- Are timeline anchors trackable in the back half?

## 18. Domain plausibility

- Would one wrong medical, legal, technical, or institutional detail break reader trust?
- Have plot-critical claims been reviewed or at least flagged for expert review?
- Are welfare, crisis, hospital, security, or governance escalations credible at the authority level shown?

## 19. Submission readiness

- Is the word count verified?
- Does the title page match the actual manuscript?
- Are code fences, markdown artifacts, and drafting residue removed?
- Is the format clearly a submission manuscript, ARC, or typeset interior?

## Fast fail triggers

If any of these are true, stop and revise:

- the same thematic insight is restated across multiple chapters without escalation
- the middle depends on repeated withholding and one-more-hidden-layer reveals
- technical beats live only on screens
- major interventions have no visible authority chain
- technical terms pile up before the reader has a plain mental model
- major characters sound interchangeable under pressure
- a harmed character functions mainly as proof for the protagonist's learning arc
- the opposition is wrong about everything
- the climax depends on a mechanism the reader cannot track
- the ending is morally right but commercially soft
- continuity on names, terms, or key facts is drifting

## Best single question

If you only ask one question, ask this:

> Is the manuscript converting its idea into varied dramatic consequence, or is it repeatedly explaining the same idea in smarter language?

# The Memory Orchard PRD

## Book identity

- Working title: The Memory Orchard
- Workflow mode: novel
- Language: English
- Target length: 82,000 words
- Standalone or series: standalone with optional sequel room

## Core concept and reader promise

A near-future literary mystery about a municipal memory archive that starts returning memories residents never donated. The reader promise is a grounded speculative mystery with emotional consequence, institutional pressure, and an ending that resolves the external case while leaving moral residue.

## Target reader and market

Primary reader: adult speculative mystery readers who like intimate stakes, institutional secrets, and morally complicated technology. Comparable adjacency: Station Eleven for humane speculative residue, The Need for intimate dread, and Slow Horses for institutional friction, but the prose should be quieter and less quippy.

## Voice, taste, and creative sovereignty

The prose should be restrained, specific, and slightly dry. It should avoid therapy-speak, glossy thriller banter, faux-poetic metaphors, and tidy speeches about memory. Preserve awkward silences, half-finished apologies, bureaucratic absurdity, and the protagonist's controlled irritation.

## Structure and scope

Three-act structure with 28-34 chapters. Act I opens with a false memory mailed to the protagonist. Act II escalates from private anomaly to public archive failure. Act III forces a public decision about whether painful false memories should be deleted, marked, or preserved.

## Characters and opposition

Main character: Mara Venn, municipal archivist, competent but conflict-avoidant. Opposition: Archive Authority, not evil, protecting civic stability and consent protocols. Secondary pressure: Mara's estranged brother, whose missing donation record may implicate the archive.

## Research, canon, and constraints

Needs plausible municipal governance, archival ethics, data-retention practices, consent logic, and public-records procedure. No fake citations in manuscript. Research can be summarized in project notes.

## Success criteria

The book succeeds if the mystery escalates cleanly, Mara makes harder choices instead of only discovering information, the institution has a coherent positive case, and the ending feels externally decisive but morally unsettled.

## Risk budget

Intentional risks: quiet pace, moral ambiguity, unresolved emotional residue. Unacceptable failures: generic evil AI/institution, protagonist passivity, pseudo-lyrical memory metaphors, padding through repeated archive visits, or an ending that becomes a lesson.

## Open questions

- How bleak should Mara's final relationship with her brother be?
- Should false memories have any physical side effects, or remain psychological/social only?
